Lead Story

OpenAI Offers Washington a 5% Stake Worth $42.6B — and Wants Every Major US Lab to Do the Same

The Financial Times reported this week that OpenAI has proposed handing the US government a 5% equity stake — worth roughly $42.6B at its post-money $852B valuation — with Sam Altman pitching the idea as the seed of an Alaska Permanent Fund-style vehicle that would let the public share directly in AI's upside. Altman is reportedly pushing for every leading US AI lab to contribute 5% into the same fund, turning a single company's political gesture into a proposed industry norm.

The timing is not incidental: it lands days after Washington delayed the public rollout of GPT-5.6 on national-security grounds and amid growing scrutiny of OpenAI's IPO ambitions and its compute-spending commitments. Coverage from Bloomberg, CNBC, CNN, Forbes, and Time all frame this as the clearest sign yet that frontier AI companies expect direct government financial entanglement — not just regulation — to be the price of continued political cover. Implementing it would likely require an act of Congress, so this remains a proposal, not a done deal — but it's the most consequential AI-policy story of the day precisely because of what it signals about where the industry thinks this is heading.

Models & Releases

Meituan Open-Sources LongCat-2.0, a 1.6T-Parameter Model Trained Entirely on Chinese Chips — Now Leading OpenRouter Traffic

Chinese delivery-and-AI conglomerate Meituan released LongCat-2.0 under an MIT license: 1.6 trillion total parameters with a mixture-of-experts design that keeps active compute to ~48B params per token, a 1M-token context window, and — the headline claim — a training run done entirely on a 50,000-card domestic Ascend 910 cluster, with zero US hardware involved. Independent trackers have it leading OpenRouter usage for agentic coding this week, and outlets from SCMP to VentureBeat are treating it as proof that Chinese labs can now hit frontier scale without Nvidia chips, four days after release.

The Frontier Race Goes Behind Closed Doors: GPT-5.6 Sol Stays Government-Gated While Grok 4.5 Runs in Secret at SpaceX and Tesla

Two of the industry's most capable new models are both, for different reasons, invisible to the public. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol — its strongest cybersecurity model yet, competitive with Anthropic's Mythos Preview on ExploitBench using a third of the output tokens — is now deploying on Cerebras hardware at up to 750 tokens/second, but access stays limited to vetted partners after the Trump administration asked OpenAI to stagger the public release. Meanwhile Musk says Grok 4.5, built on xAI's 1.5-trillion-parameter V9 base with Cursor data folded into post-training, is in private beta at SpaceX and Tesla with "performance close to, perhaps exceeding Opus" — a claim no outside party can currently verify, since the public xAI API still runs on the older Grok 4.3.

Tooling

Cursor's New Pricing Takes Effect — Split Usage Pools, a $120/Month Premium Seat, and Developer Pushback

Cursor's restructured Teams pricing went live for new customers July 1 and starts applying to existing customers on renewal: Standard seats now split usage into separate Composer/Auto and Third-Party API pools, and a new Premium seat tier offers 5x usage at roughly 3x the cost ($120/seat/month) for developers who run agents continuously. The change lands the same week GitHub Copilot's registered-user share was reported down to 51% (from 67% a year ago) and Chinese entrant Z.ai launched ZCode to compete directly with Cursor and Claude Code — three data points pointing the same direction: the ~$10B AI-coding-tools market is growing but its flat per-seat pricing model is buckling under continuous-agent usage patterns.

Research

UN's First Independent Global AI Assessment Warns Governance Can't Keep Pace — Huang, Jassy, and Brad Smith Join Follow-On Commission

The UN Independent International Scientific Panel on AI, co-chaired by Yoshua Bengio and Maria Ressa, published its preliminary findings ahead of the Global Dialogue on AI Governance opening in Geneva July 6: more than 1 billion people now use conversational AI weekly, the US controls 75% of the compute behind the world's top 500 AI supercomputers, and the length of tasks frontier agents can reliably complete is doubling every 4–7 months with no scientific guarantee of instruction-following at scale. Adding weight days before the summit, Jensen Huang (Nvidia), Andy Jassy (Amazon), and Brad Smith (Microsoft) have now joined the UN's AI governance commission — putting three of the industry's most powerful compute and cloud figures inside the body meant to check them.

Business

Anthropic Overtakes OpenAI on Annualized Revenue for the First Time — Enterprise Bet Pays Off

Anthropic's run-rate revenue has now passed OpenAI's, with figures cited between roughly $30B (trendingtopics.eu, using conservative run-rate math) and higher marks reported elsewhere — up from about $9B at the end of 2025, more than tripling in six months. Analysts trace the reversal to strategy: Anthropic bet on enterprise and coding customers (Claude Code, API usage from Cursor-style tools) while OpenAI's revenue leans more heavily on consumer ChatGPT subscriptions. Epoch AI had projected this crossover as possible by "mid-2026" back in the spring; it's now being treated as fact rather than forecast.

Anthropic Moves to Shut Down Chinese Firms' Backdoor Access to Claude via Singapore Shells and VPNs

The FT reported that Anthropic is closing workarounds Chinese companies have used to reach Claude despite its ban on China-linked entities: Ant Group reportedly issued staff corporate accounts tied to a Singapore subsidiary, while ByteDance allegedly reimbursed engineers for personal subscriptions bought over VPN. Anthropic is now watching signals like device time zones and usage patterns to catch "transfer stations" relaying mainland-China traffic through overseas accounts. CEO Dario Amodei said in February the company had already forgone "several hundred million dollars" in revenue by enforcing the China cutoff — this is the enforcement mechanism catching up to the policy.

Culture & Discourse

"Just Not Workable": Unsealed Emails Show the Real Anthropic–Pentagon Fight Was Never About Access — It Was About Autonomous Weapons

Court filings published by Gizmodo (via a WSJ report) revealed months of emails between Dario Amodei and Pentagon undersecretary Emil Michael. Amodei's line never moved: guardrails are fine, but fully autonomous weapons and domestic surveillance are off the table. Michael pushed for coverage of "all lawful uses" and eventually told Amodei the guardrails were "just not workable" — a day before Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic a supply-chain risk, a label usually reserved for firms tied to foreign adversaries. The emails are landing as pure discourse fuel: reframing a dry procurement dispute as a very human standoff over where AI labs draw moral lines against their biggest possible customer.

Tech and Finance Jobs Are Shrinking by 28,000 a Month — and This Time the Data Backs Up the AI Blame

Bloomberg's analysis of government data shows payrolls in the finance and information sectors — where AI adoption has moved fastest — have been shrinking by an average of 28,000 jobs a month in 2026, even as the broader labor market keeps adding jobs elsewhere. Separately, tech has cut over 123,000 roles this year, with AI now the single most-cited reason in layoff announcements (an estimated 87,714 year-to-date). Bank executives at JPMorgan, Citi, and Goldman have all publicly said the technology will eliminate roles. It's the first time the sector-specific payroll data itself — not just anecdotes or CEO commentary — has lined up cleanly with the "AI is already costing jobs" narrative.

Quiet but Worth Watching

Washington Is Quietly Close to Voluntary Model-Release Standards — an Announcement Could Land Before Geneva

The FT reported the US government is in advanced talks with AI companies — including Google — over voluntary standards for releasing new frontier models, with benchmarks, release timelines, and access rules for who can use them domestically and abroad. An announcement is reportedly possible "as soon as next week." It's the connective tissue behind three stories already public this week — GPT-5.6 Sol's staggered rollout, Fable 5's earlier export-control suspension and reinstatement, and Trump's June 2 executive order asking for 30-day voluntary pre-release review — but it's gotten a fraction of the coverage those individual episodes received. If it lands before the Geneva Global Dialogue on AI Governance opens July 6, it would give the US a concrete domestic framework to bring to a summit the UN's own scientific panel says the world isn't ready for.
