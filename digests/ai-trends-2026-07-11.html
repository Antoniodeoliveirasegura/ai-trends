Apple Sues OpenAI, Alleging Its Hardware Chief Ran a Recruiting Pipeline for Apple's Secrets

Apple filed suit against OpenAI in federal court in Northern California on July 10, accusing it of a systematic scheme to steal trade secrets for OpenAI's unannounced consumer hardware. The complaint centers on hardware chief Tang Tan, a former Apple VP who led iPhone and Watch design — Apple alleges he directed job candidates still employed at Apple to bring "actual parts" to interviews for "show and tell," used Apple's internal project code names while recruiting, and coached departing staff on evading Apple's security procedures. It also accuses a former engineer, Chang Liu, of retaining access to Apple's internal cloud storage after leaving, downloading confidential files, and stealing a company laptop.

It matters because this is the clearest signal yet that OpenAI is building consumer hardware, and it's the first head-on legal collision between two companies that went from App Store partners (ChatGPT is baked into iOS) to open rivals. It also lands one day after news outlets asked a federal judge to sanction OpenAI in the NYT copyright case — a second major suit hitting OpenAI in the same week, reinforcing a pattern of its recruiting and data practices drawing legal fire from multiple directions at once. OpenAI says it has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets."
