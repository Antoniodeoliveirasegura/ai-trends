Google Loses Its Two Most Storied AI Researchers in Three Days — Shazeer to OpenAI, Nobel Laureate Jumper to Anthropic

Between June 18 and 19, Google lost the two most symbolically loaded names in its AI portfolio: Noam Shazeer — co-author of the 2017 "Attention Is All You Need" paper and Gemini's co-lead — announced he is joining OpenAI, less than two years after Google paid roughly $2.7 billion to bring him back from Character.AI. The very next day, John Jumper, the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry laureate and head of AlphaFold, confirmed he is leaving DeepMind for Anthropic after nearly nine years. The back-to-back exits landed simultaneously on Fortune, Axios, and Search Engine Journal on June 23, setting the tone for today's discourse.

Why it matters: these aren't ordinary attrition numbers — they are the names attached to the transformer architecture and to protein-structure prediction, respectively. Their departures validate a pattern that analysts have flagged for months: frontier researchers are choosing well-funded challengers over Big Tech divisions, drawn by startup-like focus and equity upside that internal roles cannot match. Alphabet shares dropped on the news, and questions are now being asked in earnest about whether DeepMind can remain at the frontier.

EU's EUROPA Consortium Wins Frontier AI Grand Challenge — Sovereign Open-Source 400B-Parameter Model in All 24 EU Languages

The European Commission selected the EUROPA Consortium on June 19 as winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge, backed by a 6,000-chip NVIDIA Blackwell cluster. The mandate: a sovereign, open-source model exceeding 400 billion parameters, trained natively across all 24 official EU languages. It is getting almost no coverage outside EU policy circles, but the project represents the most serious state-backed challenge to US AI dominance to date — and the first major frontier model effort that is explicitly multilingual by design, not as an afterthought. If it ships, it resets what "sovereign AI" means as a category.
