OpenAI Pitches the US Government a 5% Stake in Every Major AI Lab — Altman's "Alaska Permanent Fund" Play

The Financial Times and Bloomberg reported today that Sam Altman has proposed Washington take a 5% equity stake in OpenAI — worth roughly $42.6B at its $852B valuation — as part of a broader arrangement that would extend the same terms to Anthropic, Google, and Meta, structured like a sovereign fund that pays dividends to the public. Altman has reportedly been pitching the concept to the Trump administration since early 2025, but it surfaces now as political blowback over AI-linked layoffs and export-control fights intensifies.

It's the story of the day because it reframes the entire "AI vs. government" relationship: rather than resisting oversight, OpenAI is offering Washington a direct financial stake in the industry's upside — right as it and Anthropic are separately gearing up for IPOs under heightened regulatory scrutiny. Anthropic has already confirmed it isn't part of these talks, and it's unclear Google or Meta would sign on, which makes this less a done deal than a bid to set the terms of the next phase of AI policy.

Study: AI Impersonations of Politicians Rated More "Authentic" Than the Real Thing

Published in PLOS One on July 1, the study fed GPT-4 Turbo the Wikipedia biographies of 112 public figures and had it answer real audience questions from 30 episodes of BBC's Question Time. A representative sample of 948 Britons rated the AI-generated answers as more authentic, relevant, and coherent than what the actual politicians said — despite measurable differences in vocabulary and hedging language. A blunt data point for anyone worried about AI-driven political impersonation ahead of upcoming election cycles.

Quiet but Worth Watching

"Phantom Squatting": Attackers Are Registering Domains That AI Chatbots Simply Made Up

Unit 42 built a 2.1-million-URL corpus of domains hallucinated by LLMs and found 13,229 already weaponized for malware or phishing — two-thirds of it malware delivery. The mechanism is quiet but corrosive: when a model invents a URL and confidently hands it to a user or an autonomous agent, whoever registers that exact string first inherits the model's credibility, no phishing email required. Researchers say the hallucination-to-registration window runs 18-51 days, meaning there's a real, if narrow, chance to defend against it — but almost nobody outside security research is tracking it yet.
