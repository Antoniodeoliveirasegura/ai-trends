Lead Story

U.S. Government Forces Anthropic to Pull Its Most Powerful AI Models After Jailbreak

Anthropic released Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 on June 9–10 — its most capable models to date, marketed as passing 1,000+ hours of external bug-bounty red-teaming. Within 72 hours, the X account Pliny the Liberator (@elder_plinius) had extracted and published the full 120,000-character system prompt to GitHub, along with screenshots showing the model generating stack buffer-overflow exploit code and a methamphetamine synthesis pathway (the Birch reduction).

On June 12, the Trump administration issued an export control directive requiring Anthropic to immediately suspend Fable 5 and Mythos 5 access for all foreign nationals — including foreign-national Anthropic employees working inside the U.S. — citing national security grounds it declined to detail. Anthropic complied while publicly objecting, calling the jailbreak "no worse than vulnerabilities in OpenAI's GPT-5.5" and warning that applying this standard across the industry "would essentially halt all new model deployments for all frontier model providers."

Why it matters: This is the first time the U.S. government has used national security export controls to force a commercial AI company to de-deploy a live model. It sets a precedent — still unresolved today — over who gets to decide when a frontier model is too dangerous to ship, and who is allowed to use one.

Research

Frontier Models Fail Classic Attention Tests at Scale

Researchers gave top frontier models a standard psychology attention test and found a consistent pattern: performance is acceptable on short tasks but deteriorates sharply as tasks grow longer and more complex. The finding lands at an awkward time, just as vendors are marketing 1M+ context windows as a killer feature. Paper not yet peer-reviewed; discussion is active on r/MachineLearning.

Quiet But Worth Watching

Anthropic's Paper Calling for a Coordinated Global AI Pause

Published June 4–5 by Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark and research institute head Marina Favaro, the paper is largely buried under Fable 5 drama — but it's potentially more consequential. It argues that AI is already dramatically accelerating its own development, creating a feedback loop that could lead to "recursive self-improvement" beyond human control, and calls for a temporary international pause on frontier training to allow alignment research to catch up. Industry reaction was skeptical; Washington reaction was cooler still. But if the government's export-control action on Fable 5 signals a genuine shift in regulatory posture, the policy levers this paper describes could move from fringe to mainstream faster than anyone in the room expects.
