Lead Story

OpenAI's Offer of a Government Stake Runs Into a Bipartisan Buzzsaw

A day after the Financial Times revealed Sam Altman's proposal to hand Washington a 5% OpenAI stake (~$42.6B) via a sovereign-wealth-style fund, the reaction landed from both directions at once: former Trump strategist Steve Bannon dismissed it as "tip money" and demanded 50% instead, while watchdog group Public Knowledge warned that a government that's also a shareholder has every incentive to go easy on safety regulation — the same "too big to fail" logic critics leveled at 2008 bank bailouts. Senator Bernie Sanders piled on with a rival bill, the American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act, proposing a mandatory 50% stock tax on AI firms over $200M in revenue to seed a fund its office estimates at $7 trillion, paying every American $1,000+ a year.

It matters because the fight has quietly moved past "should the public get a cut of AI's gains" to "how big a cut, structured how" — with the far right, the socialist left, and the industry itself all now negotiating over the same mechanism. That's happening in parallel with the White House finalizing separate voluntary standards for frontier-model releases, meaning equity-sharing and pre-release oversight are converging into a single, fast-moving AI-policy bargain.

Quiet but Worth Watching

A Model's "Personality" Might Be a Trick of Question Order, Not a Fixed Trait

A new arXiv paper testing GPT-4o personas on the IPIP-50 psychometric battery finds that a persona's aggregate traits (its Big Five scores) hold up reasonably well when questions are reordered, but the underlying geometric structure of how those traits relate to each other collapses by 42% under the same reordering — then mostly recovers if both sides share the same question frame. The implication is quietly significant for anyone building persona-conditioned agents, synthetic survey respondents, or "digital twins": persona consistency may be an artifact of prompt structure rather than something genuinely stable inside the model, and almost no one outside a narrow ML-research circle is discussing it yet.
