OpenAI and Broadcom Unveil "Jalapeño" — OpenAI's First Custom AI Chip, Built in Nine Months, Targets Gigawatt-Scale Deployment by End of 2026

Announced June 24, OpenAI and Broadcom jointly revealed Jalapeño, an LLM-optimized inference accelerator co-developed from initial design to tape-out in roughly nine months — believed to be the fastest ASIC development cycle ever achieved in high-performance advanced semiconductors. Engineering samples were physically delivered to OpenAI leadership on June 24. Jalapeño is purpose-built for inference (running models in response to user commands, not training), and early testing shows substantially better performance per watt than current state-of-the-art. Critically, OpenAI's own models were used to accelerate portions of the chip design process itself — a recursive loop that underscores how AI is now eating its own supply chain.

Why it matters: Jalapeño is OpenAI's clearest move yet to reduce dependence on Nvidia for inference at scale. It is the first chip in a multi-generation platform, with gigawatt-scale deployment in Microsoft data centers targeted before December 31, 2026. The Hacker News thread immediately became one of the most-commented AI stories this week. If Jalapeño hits its performance-per-watt targets in production, it signals that hyperscalers and frontier labs can genuinely build their way out of Nvidia's inference pricing — a structural shift that changes the economics of every token served.

Major AI Conference Sets LLM Watermark Trap — Catches 497 Peer Reviewers Using AI to Write Reviews

Nature reported June 25 that a top AI conference rejected 497 submitted papers — roughly 2% of all submissions — after organizers embedded hidden-text watermarks in review packages instructing any LLM processing them to include telltale phrases in the written review. Reviewers who piped the submission through an LLM instead of reading it themselves unwittingly revealed the shortcut. The sting follows a separate Pangram AI estimate that 21% of ICLR 2026 reviews contained LLM-generated text. The finding compounds earlier scandals: 100 fabricated citations appeared in NeurIPS 2025 papers that cleared peer review, and a separate investigation found 94 fake reviewer identities. The structural integrity of AI peer review is now an open question.
