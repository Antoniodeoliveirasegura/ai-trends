Anthropic Says It Found a "Global Workspace" Inside Claude — and the Internet Can't Decide What That Means

A 16-author Anthropic interpretability paper published this week identifies "J-space" — a small, privileged band of internal neural activity, found using a Jacobian-based technique, where Claude holds and manipulates a few dozen concepts at a time before they ever reach text. Suppressing it barely dents simple classification but collapses multi-step reasoning, recall, and creative generation, and the researchers show it satisfies five functional properties neuroscientists associate with conscious access in humans — without claiming that proves anything about experience.

It matters because it's the most rigorous empirical bridge yet built between mechanistic interpretability and consciousness science, arriving the same week Anthropic's own "hidden workspace" framing hit mainstream tech press — and it's already splitting reactions between "this is a landmark for understanding how LLMs actually think" and Gizmodo's blunter warning not to read the paper uncritically as evidence of machine sentience. Either way, it's reset the terms of the AI-consciousness debate from vibes to a testable mechanism.
