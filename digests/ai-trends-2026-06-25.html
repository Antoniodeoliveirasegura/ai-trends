Reflection AI Signs $6.3 Billion Compute Deal with SpaceX — America's Open-Weight Frontier Bet Is Now Fully Capitalized

Announced June 22 via CNBC, Reflection AI — the $25 billion New York startup founded by ex-DeepMind researchers Misha Laskin and Ioannis Antonoglou — has signed a three-year, $6.3 billion compute agreement with SpaceX, securing access to Nvidia GB300 chips at SpaceX's Colossus 2 data center near Memphis beginning July 1. At $150 million per month, it is the single largest known compute contract in open-source AI history, and the most concrete signal yet that Reflection's first public frontier training run is imminent.

Why it matters: Reflection is explicitly positioned as the American answer to DeepSeek and Alibaba's Qwen — open-weight, permissively licensed, and built for government and national security customers the closed labs won't fully serve. The deal also deepens SpaceX's rapid transformation into an AI infrastructure empire: in the same week it closed the $60 billion Cursor acquisition, SpaceX's Colossus data center now anchors the compute stack for one of the most-watched frontier training runs of 2026. If Nvidia is on both sides of the trade — supplying the chips and backing Reflection as an investor — the structural concentration of AI infrastructure is tightening faster than most observers expected.

ICLR 2026 Paper Documents 99% Bypass Rate for "Abliteration" — Open-Weight Safety Alignment Has a Structural Flaw

The technique that powers the Heretic tool — "abliteration," a portmanteau of ablation and obliteration — received its first peer-reviewed treatment at ICLR 2026. A published paper documents a refined version achieving up to a 99% bypass rate on tested open-weight models including Meta's Llama 3.3 and Google's Gemma 3. The core finding: current safety alignment trains refusal behavior into isolated, identifiable neural pathways rather than integrating it throughout the model, making targeted surgical removal fast and reliable on a consumer laptop in under ten minutes. Over 3,500 "decensored" variants have been distributed with 13 million total downloads. The paper argues that unless alignment is structurally redesigned, fine-tuning-based safety will remain cosmetic on open-weight models.

Great American AI Act Discussion Draft: 269 Pages, 3-Year State Preemption, Mandatory Audits for Frontier Labs

Representatives Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released a 269-page discussion draft on June 4, billing it as the most comprehensive federal AI framework ever put forward by Congress. Key provisions: states retain the right to regulate AI use within their borders but lose the power to legislate how AI systems are built — a 3-year preemption that overrides California's AB 1047, the EU AI Act, and over a dozen state bills. Separately, "large frontier developers" (those with $500M+ annual AI revenue) would face binding disclosure requirements, mandatory third-party audits, and whistleblower protections. The draft has drawn immediate fire from Public Citizen and consumer advocates who say it strips state-level protections; industry broadly welcomes the preemption. The discussion period is ongoing — this is not yet a bill.

Anthropic in Early Talks to Run Claude on Microsoft's Maia 200 Chips — Custom Silicon's First Frontier Test

CNBC reported May 21 that Anthropic is in early discussions to lease Azure servers running Microsoft's second-generation Maia 200 AI accelerator — a chip built exclusively for AI inference on TSMC's 3nm process and already deployed in Microsoft's Arizona and Iowa data centers for GPT-5.2 workloads. No deal has been signed, and Anthropic currently spreads inference across Google Cloud TPUs and AWS Trainium/Inferentia2. But if this closes, it would be the first time a frontier model made by one lab runs production inference on custom silicon built by a different company. The precedent matters: it would validate a fully chip-decoupled inference supply chain and accelerate every lab's ability to walk away from Nvidia for inference-heavy workloads.
