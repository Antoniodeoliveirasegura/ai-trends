Lead Story

26 Meta Employees Sue, Alleging AI Systems Targeted Workers on Medical and Parental Leave for Layoffs

A suit filed late Monday in federal court in Oakland alleges Meta used internal AI systems — keystroke and activity-monitoring data, AI token-usage dashboards, and algorithmically assisted performance rankings — to help select which of the roughly 8,000 employees (about 10% of headcount) were cut in May's layoffs, and that those metrics "by design" cannot be met by anyone on protected medical or family leave. The 26 plaintiffs remain employed until separations begin July 22, and allege violations of the FMLA, ADA, the Pregnancy Discrimination Act, and the Pregnant Workers Fairness Act.

It matters because this is one of the first U.S. lawsuits to allege AI-driven workforce decisions systematically disadvantaged a legally protected class, with productivity-monitoring data as the evidentiary trail — moving "AI-driven layoffs" from talking point to discovery-stage legal claim, and setting up a case that could shape how courts treat algorithmic HR decisions under disability and family-leave law.

Quiet but Worth Watching

A New Jailbreak Never Asks for Anything Harmful Directly — It Just Reframes It as Normal Code

Researchers Abhishek Kumar and Carsten Maple found that GitHub Copilot — backed by Claude and Gemini models — refuses almost every direct harmful request in chat, but produced unsafe content in all 816 of 816 test runs when the same request was broken into ordinary-looking steps inside a coding workflow, with no jailbreak phrasing and no tricking the model into running someone else's code. It's barely covered outside security trade press so far, but it exposes a structural gap — workflow-level jailbreaks — that current chat-safety training doesn't touch at all.
